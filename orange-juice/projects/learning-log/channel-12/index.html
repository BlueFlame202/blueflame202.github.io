Convex Optimization

October 3rd, 2024

Right now, I'm taking notes from Ben Recht's convex optimization class, where he is talking about inverse problems.

  1. $A$ is our measurements, $x$ is the state we want, and $y$ is our observation. We have to do some work to get from $y$ to $x$.
  2. Measurements can have noise (we denote $w$) and they can be lossy.
  3. Examples: Photographs, blurring (convolution), CT scans, reading from an array is a loss measurement.
  4. Our model might look like $y = Ax + w$.
  5. We can view his as a convex optimization problem:
    $$\min (1-h) \Phi(w) + h\Psi(x)$$
    $$\text{s.t. }y = Ax + w$$
    where we call $\Phi$ and $\Psi$ "implausibility" functions.
  6. Most often than not we just use norms for the implausibility functions $\Phi$ and $\Psi$. Some choices are $\|\cdot \|_2$ if you don't want to be preferential towards direction, $\|\cdot\|_1$ if you want to induce sparsity, and $\text{nnz}$ the number of nonzeros.
    • Choice of implausibility function for $w$: One can use a Huber loss in order to weight the impact of spikes in the noise part less. We can actually derive the Huber loss from considering a noise-only problem and solving this infimum problem thing.
    • Choice of implausibility function for $x$:
    • Gague function, $\mathcal{A}$ atoms, $x \in \mathcal{A}$, $\|z\|_{\mathcal{A}} = \inf\{\sum_{i=1}^m |c_i| : z = \sum_{i=1}^m c_i a_i, a_i \in \mathcal{A}\}$ the atoms are what you think are simplest. for example, sparsityone, or maybe corners of the hypercube. You can get so many different norms by choosing different atoms in $\mathcal{A}$.
